Particle Swarm more_vert
Particle Swarm close

Using the cursor as a target, the particles in the swarm converge towards the cursor using a genetic algorithm. A typical genetic algorithm is made up of three parts.

Selection: Each generation, or iteration of the algorithm, the particle closest to the target is determined.

Crossover: Next, the other particles move towards the best particle by changing their position to be the crossover of the best particle and their current position.

Mutation: During the crossover process, there is a random chance of a particles position to get a random offset. This is called mutation and though may not seem desirable it is crucial. Without mutation, the particles would converge on the particle closest to the target and never move, even if the target is moved. This occurs, since the particles are converging to best particle, not the target, so if all particles share the same position, nothing happens.

Given an NxN board, we need to arange N Queens on a chess board such that no queen can capture another. For example, for N=8 (As in the picture) 8 queens are aranged on a 8x8 chess board such that no queen can capture another.
